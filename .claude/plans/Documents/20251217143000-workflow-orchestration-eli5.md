## The Big Picture: What Problem Does This Solve?

Imagine you're a **restaurant manager**. When a customer places an order:

1. Kitchen makes the food
2. Waiter brings it to the table
3. Cashier prepares the bill
4. Host thanks them on the way out

You don't want the **waiter** to know how to cook, or the **cashier** to serve food. Each person does ONE job. But someone needs to **coordinate** them in the right order.

**Your orchestrator is that coordinator.**

### The "visibility timeout" magic

When the orchestrator picks up a ticket, it becomes *invisible* for 60 seconds. If the orchestrator crashes, the ticket *reappears* automatically and gets picked up again. No lost orders!

## Summary: The Cast of Characters

| Component | Role | Restaurant Analogy |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| `workflow_definitions` | Recipe book | Menu with cooking instructions |
| `workflow-enqueue` | Order taker | Waiter writing tickets |
| `workflow_queue` (pgmq) | Ticket rail | Wire holding order tickets |
| `workflow-orchestrator` | Chef coordinator | Head chef calling out orders |
| `workflow_executions` | Order history | Receipt/audit trail |
| `send-email`, `slack`, etc. | Line cooks | Individual stations doing one job |

---

## Key Benefits

1. **Frontend** makes ONE call and gets an immediate response
2. **Individual Edge Functions** (send-email, slack) stay simple â€” they just do ONE thing
3. **Orchestrator** handles the complexity of coordination, retries, and tracking
4. **Database** stores all the configuration â€” change workflows without code deploys
5. **Queue** provides durability â€” no lost work even if servers crash
